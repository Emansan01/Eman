Origins of Origami

Origami, the ancient art of paper folding, has its roots deeply embedded in Japanese culture. The word "origami" itself is a fusion of two Japanese terms: "ori," meaning to fold, and "kami," meaning paper. While the exact origin remains somewhat elusive, origami is believed to have evolved in Japan during the Edo period (1603-1868). Initially, it was associated with ceremonial practices and was considered a luxury reserved for the elite. Over time, origami became more accessible to the general population, evolving from traditional forms to a dynamic, creative expression embraced worldwide. Today, origami encompasses a vast array of shapes, from simple animals to complex geometric designs, and continues to captivate enthusiasts of all ages.

How to make a paper crane

To embark on the delightful journey of crafting a paper crane, begin with a rectangular sheet of paper. If the paper isn't already square, take a moment to trim it into a square shape by cutting off the excess. Once you have your square canvas, fold it diagonally, bringing opposite corners together to form a triangle. Unfold and repeat this process with the remaining corners, creating an "X" on the paper.

Next, flip the paper, and fold it in half both horizontally and vertically, forming a smaller square. Unfurl the paper to reveal intersecting creases, akin to a grid. Now, bring the corners of this square to the center, creating a smaller square. Rotate the paper and fold each corner to the center once more. The paper now takes on the semblance of a kite.

Proceed to fold the top layer's edges to the centerline, giving the paper a distinct diamond shape. To complete the iconic crane form, fold the sides downward, shaping the paper into the graceful silhouette of a crane.
